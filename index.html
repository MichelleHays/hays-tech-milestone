The World Brain

The World Brain was created by H.G. Wells in 1934. It's the first invention to use hyperlinks like the ones used in modern technology.
It is a collection of essays and addresses. It was a massive search engine, with well over 15 millions entries.
Also referred to as the World Encyclopedia, Wells wanted it to be free and considered it his contribution
to world peace. He was inspired around the time of the first world war, feeling that people needed to be more educated.
His invention paved the way for hyperlinks and modern day information accessibility. source

The Z3 Computer

In 1935, Konrad Zuse began designing the Z3 computer, completing the task in 1941. The Z3 computer is considered
to be the first automatic digital computer. The computer's program code was stored on punched film, and initial values were
put in manually. Unfortunatley, the computer was destroyed on December 21, 1943. A replica of the Z3 computer was later built
in 1961, and is displayed in a museum. This invention was a stepping stone towards more modern computers, which is why Zuse is
often credited with having invented the computer. source

The Atanasoff-Berry Computer

The Atanasoff-Berry Computer is the first electronic computer. Professor John Vincent Atanasoff receives funds in 1939,
planning to create this computer at what was Iowa State College at the time. The machine was completed in 1942 by Atanasoff and a partner.
A legal battle over the patent for the invention of the computer was brought about, and later solved in 1973. What's important about this
battle, and it's revolution, was that computers were agreed to be unpatentable. This means that computers can be created and profited by freely
by anyone who wanted to create one. This made the computers advancement everyone's ability. source

A Logic Named Joe

In 1946, Will F, Jenkins' A Logic Named Joe was published in an issue of Astounding Science Fiction. Jenkins' book captured
the capabilities of computers. An impressive science fiction, it follows the story of "logics" which are computer-like devices. Although much ahead
of it's time, this book successfully predicted the massive networks of personal computers, and the ability to search for information, including their
drawbacks, like people finding information that was supposed to be secret. With the understanding of the technology in Jenkin's book, it can be said
that he was the first to have the idea of a massive network, and likeley paved the way for it to be made. source

The Manchester Baby

On June 21, 1948, the Small-Scale Experimental Machine (SSEM), created by researchers at the University of Manchester, ran it's first program.
It's inventors, Frederic Williams, Tom Kilburn, and Geoff Toothill, intended for the machine not to be a practical computer, but instead was made to
test a new memory technology, the Williams tube. Though primitive, it was the first working machine to contain all essential elements of a modern computer.
It was very important to the development in the modern day computer, and the development of memory. source
